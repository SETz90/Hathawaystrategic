The Mark

James Denney came to us with a consultancy that had the expertise but not yet the presence: a construction and architecture practice competing for attention in a crowded Nashville market without a visual identity to match its craftsmanship. He needed a mark that could sit on a hard hat and a hotel signage panel and feel like the same company in both places.

We built the identity around three ideas that had to work together in a single shape: the company's initials, the relationship at the center of its service model, and the solidity of the industry it serves.

  • J, P, D are interlocked directly into the form rather than set as separate characters, so the initials read as one continuous silhouette instead of three stacked letters.

  • Two shapes meet at the center of the mark to suggest two hands in contact — a visual shorthand for the help and service at the center of how JPD works with clients.

  • The outer geometry is built from block-like facets that echo massing diagrams and structural framing, grounding the mark in construction rather than abstract branding.

The Challenge

A strong consulting practice with no cohesive way to present itself is invisible next to competitors with polished branding, however thin. JPD needed an identity system that could stand up in front of institutional clients, a website that could carry its portfolio of past work, and a set of physical touchpoints — from business cards to job-site signage — that all trace back to the same idea without looking like they were designed by three different agencies.

The Strategy

We treated the logo as the root system rather than a single asset. Once the mark, palette, and typography were locked, every deliverable — stationery, apparel, signage, editorial — was built as an application of that same root rather than a fresh design decision.

Charcoal navy and construction orange carry the palette: navy for the steel and stability of the trade, orange for visibility and energy on-site — the same contrast you'd see on a hard hat or a crane arm.

In the Field

Applications

The system was designed to travel: from a media kit to a street-level billboard, the same mark, palette, and voice hold up at every scale.

The Outcome

JPD Consulting now carries one visual language across every surface it touches — a mark that reads as construction-grade and a website built to showcase its project history to institutional clients. The identity gives a growing Nashville consultancy the same shelf presence as firms many times its size.
